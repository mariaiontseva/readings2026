Vyāsabhāṣya
L
Leggett's continuous translation ▾
The word Now means here a beginning, and the topic now begun is understood to be an exposition of yoga. Yoga (yogaḥ) is samādhi (samādhiḥ). It is a quality (dharma) of the mind in all the five mental states: impulsive, dull, changeable, one-pointed, inhibited. In the changeable state of the mind, the samādhi is subject to this changeability, and is not classed as yoga. But the samādhi in the one-pointed mind makes clear the object as it is, destroys the taints, loosens the karma-bonds, and brings the state of inhibition into view; it is called cognitive. It follows on meditations with verbal associations (vitarka), with subtle associations (vicāra), accompanied with joy, and accompanied with I-am-ness; we shall speak of it later. But the ultra-cognitive (samādhi) is when there is inhibition of all mental processes.

Vyāsabhāṣya
L
Leggett's continuous translation ▾
The omission of the word All shows that the cognitive (samādhi) too is yoga. Now mind always tends towards illumination, activity, or stasis (sthiti); so it consists of the three guṇa-s. For mind-sattva is by nature illumination. Mingled with rajas and tamas, mind is drawn towards power and possessions. Pervaded by tamas, it becomes subject to a-dharma, Ignorance, attachment and helplessness. That same mind, when the covering of illusion (moha) has dwindled, pervaded by a measure of rajas, is endowed with dharma, knowledge (jñāna), detachment and power. When the last trace of the stain of rajas is removed, the mind is grounded in its own nature, becoming simply the Knowledge (khyāti) that sattva and Puruṣa are different, endowed with the Rain-cloud of Dharma, which the meditators call the highest prasaṃkhyāna. The power-of-consciousness (citi-śakti) is unchangeable, does not engage with objects, has the objects shown to it, is pure and infinite. Opposed to it is the Knowledge-of-the-difference (viveka-khyāti), whose nature is sattva-guṇa. Therefore the mind, turning away from even that Knowledge, inhibits it. In this state what remains is saṃskāra-s, and it is the seedless (nirbīja) samādhi. There is no cognition of anything in it, so it is ultra-cognitive.

It is mainly concerned with determining a universal. As having motion is inferred in the moon and stars, from their getting to another place, as with the man Caitra. The Vindhya range has no movement because it does not get anywhere. Authority (āgama): when someone competent communicates by word something seen or inferred by him, with the intention of conveying his own knowledge, the mental process whose object is the meaning obtained from the word is authority for the hearer. When a speaker says questionable things, neither seen nor inferred by himself, this is fallacious authority. But if an original speaker has seen or inferred it, the authority is not suspect.

Vyāsabhāṣya
L
Leggett's continuous translation ▾
Is it the object, or the thought of it, that the mind remembers? The thought, coloured by the thing known, shines out in the forms both of the thing and the knowledge of it, and sets up a saṃskāra of the same kind. That manifests in accordance with its manifesting cause. The saṃskāra gives rise to a memory: cognition has primarily the form of the knowing experience; memory has primarily the form of the thing known. The latter is of two kinds: where the things remembered are actualized, and where they are not. In dream the things remembered become actual, but at the time of waking such things are not actualized. All memories arise from experience of right knowledge, illusion, logical construction, sleep, or other memories, and all these are essentially pleasure, pain and delusion. Pleasure, pain and delusion are to be explained as taints: Desire is the consequence of pleasure (II.7), Hate is the consequence of pain (II.8), Delusion is Ignorance (II.5). All these mental processes are to be inhibited. When they are inhibited there is either cognitive or ultra-cognitive samādhi.

Practised for a long time, practised uninterruptedly, practised with reverence — carried through with austerity, with brahmacarya, with knowledge and with faith, in reverence, it becomes firmly grounded. The meaning is that the purpose is not suddenly overwhelmed by an extravertive saṃskāra.
1
dīrghakālāsevito nirantarāsevitaḥ satkārāsevitaḥ.
Translation
Cultivated for a long time (dīrgha-kāla-āsevitaḥ), cultivated without interruption (nirantara-āsevitaḥ), cultivated with devotion (satkāra-āsevitaḥ).
Объяснение
Вьяса разбивает компаунд на три самостоятельных условия. Каждое повторяет āsevita.
2

Accomplished (saṃpāditaḥ) through austerity, celibacy, knowledge, and faith (tapasā brahmacaryeṇa vidyayā śraddhayā ca) — [that which is] possessing devotion (satkāravān) becomes (bhavati) firmly grounded (dṛḍha-bhūmiḥ).

Vyāsabhāṣya
L
Leggett's continuous translation ▾
Detachment is the consciousness of self-mastery of one who has no thirst for any object either seen or heard about. It is that consciousness in one who is unmoved by visible objects like women, food and drink, or power, who is without thirst for objects heard about such as attainment of heaven or the state of the gods or of those absorbed into prakṛti, who is inwardly aware of the defects in them by the power [of discriminative meditation], which has nothing to avoid and nothing to accept — that is detachment.
1
striyo 'nnapānam aiśvaryam iti dṛṣṭaviṣaye vitṛṣṇasya svargavaidehyaprakṛtilayatvaprāptāv ānuśravikaviṣaye vitṛṣṇasya divyādivyaviṣayasaṃprayoge 'pi cittasya viṣayadoṣadarśinaḥ prasaṃkhyānabalād anābhogātmikā heyopādeyaśūnyā vaśīkārasaṃjñā vairāgyam.
Translation
Dispassion (vairāgyam) is the consciousness of mastery (vaśīkāra-saṃjñā) — of the nature of non-engagement (anābhoga-ātmikā), devoid of "to-be-rejected" and "to-be-acquired" (heya-upādeya-śūnyā) — arising by the strength of discriminative meditation (prasaṃkhyāna-balāt) in the mind that sees the fault in objects (cittasya viṣaya-doṣa-darśinaḥ), even in contact with divine and non-divine objects (divya-adivya-viṣaya-saṃprayoge api), in one free from thirst (vitṛṣṇasya) for visible objects (dṛṣṭa-viṣaye) — namely women, food-and-drink, power (striyaḥ anna-pānam aiśvaryam iti) — and free from thirst (vitṛṣṇasya) for revealed objects (ānuśravika-viṣaye) — [namely] the attainment of heaven, bodiless state, absorption into prakṛti (svarga-vaideha-prakṛti-layatva-prāptau).

Объяснение

Два уровня отречения:

  1. Dṛṣṭaviṣaya — мирские: женщины, еда, власть.
  2. Ānuśravikaviṣaya — из писаний: небеса, бестелесность, растворение в пракрити (духовные обещания Вед).

Йогин свободен от жажды к обоим.

Механизм: ум видит изъян (viṣaya-doṣa-darśin). Не слепое подавление, а видение дефекта.

Источник: prasaṃkhyāna-bala. Результат: ум anābhoga и heyopādeyaśūnya. Высшая форма: не «я не хочу», а полное отсутствие самих категорий желания и отвержения.
