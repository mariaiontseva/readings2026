Essay 1“Tantric/Purāṇic Śiva cannot simply be the peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra.” Discuss.

¶1Thesis: the claim is right＋
“peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra”

The claim is right. The phrase “peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra” gets both ends wrong — what śiva was in the Veda, and what Tantric Śiva is (I’ll keep the Purāṇic outside).

¶2Veda: Rudra is a fearsome god＋
ṚgvedaRV 1.114 · 2.33 · 7.46RV 10.92.9Taittirīya SaṃhitāŚatarudrīyaTS 5.7.3.3TS 4.5.8 “the well-disposed, very helpful, self-glorious one”“two bodies… ghora- / śiva-”namaḥ śivāya ca śivatarāya ca Jamison & BreretonSrinivasan

Rudra in the Ṛgveda is primarily a fearsome god, cattle-slayer (goghna) and man-slayer (puruṣaghna), to be begged to turn his arrows away (RV 1.114, 2.33, 7.46). Against this baseline, śiva enters as an adjective, “kindly”, “auspicious”, and the first direct application to Rudra appears only late, in RV 10.92.9, where Jamison and Brereton render the relevant phrase as “the well-disposed, very helpful, self-glorious one.” It begins as a way of speaking to a dangerous god. By the later Vedic period this hardens into a proper aspect. The Taittirīya Saṃhitā makes the structure explicit: Rudra has “two bodies, the one fearful (ghora-), the other auspicious (śiva-)”, and the Śatarudrīya is composed specifically to pacify the fearful body while a separate offering honours the auspicious one (TS 5.7.3.3; Srinivasan). The same litany addresses Rudra by the epithet itself in namaḥ śivāya ca śivatarāya ca (TS 4.5.8). So a śiva aspect does emerge, but it never detaches. It is one pole of a ritual mechanism for handling a fearsome god, always paired with the fearful body it soothes, never a separable figure in its own right.

¶3Tantras take Rudra whole＋
Śaiva SiddhāntaTrikaPratyabhijñāVidyāpīṭhaAbhinavagupta

The Tantras then do not select the peaceful side. They take Rudra whole and develop both poles into full theology. The śiva pole becomes Sadāśiva of Śaiva Siddhānta, Paramaśiva of the Trika, the absolute consciousness of the Pratyabhijñā, Śiva as light and self-awareness (prakāśa-vimarśa). The ghora pole becomes Bhairava — the fierce cremation-ground deity of the Bhairava and Vidyāpīṭha tantras, emulated in the skull-bearing Kāpālika observance. In the great non-dualist syntheses of Abhinavagupta the two are one god, held together. What was a ritual technology in the Veda becomes a theological vision in the Tantras: not the calming of a fearsome god, but the full elaboration of his double nature.

¶4Fierce pole: pre-Tantric genealogy＋
AtharvavedaAV 15.1 “ox vow” Acharya (2013)

The fierce pole, moreover, has its own pre-Tantric genealogy. The Atharvaveda records Rudra’s worship among the Vrātyas (AV 15.1), groups outside mainstream Brahmanical culture who maintained vow-based ritual traditions. Acharya (2013) traces the Pāśupata godharma back to the Vrātya “ox vow”, elaborated in more transgressive form, with imitation of bovine behaviour and suspension of dietary and sexual taboos. What the Pāśupata–Kāpālika line inherits is not yet a fully formed fierce Śiva — that theology develops later — but the formal pattern of the antinomian vow, running alongside the mainstream cult from the start.

¶5Conclusion: double-bodied, not half＋
“peaceful Rudra” never existed

Vedic Rudra is the forerunner of Tantric Śiva, but as a double-bodied god, never as half of one. A “peaceful Rudra” never existed to be carried through.

RUСвязь параграфов — как одно вытекает из другого＋

  • ¶1→¶2Берёмся за ведийский конец. Rudra в Веде — страшный бог. Слово śiva приходит поздно и сначала это просто вежливое обращение к опасному богу. В Taittirīya Saṃhitā оно затвердевает в одну из двух телесных сторон — и эта сторона неотделима от грозной.
  • ¶2→¶3Раз структура уже парная, тантрам нечего «выбирать». Это твои же слова в ¶3: они «do not select the peaceful side; take Rudra whole». Оба полюса вырастают в теологию (śiva → Sadāśiva/Paramaśiva; ghora → Bhairava — грозное божество Bhairava/Vidyāpīṭha-тантр, которого капалики имитируют).
  • ¶3→¶4Усиливаем: у грозного полюса своя до-тантрическая родословная — он не выдумка тантр. Линия идёт от Vrātya и сохранена в Atharvaveda (через Acharya 2013), и шла рядом с мейнстримным культом с самого начала.
  • ¶4→¶5Вывод. Rudra — предшественник Tantric Śiva, но как двутелый бог, а не как половина одного. «Мирного Rudra» не существовало вовсе — нечему было «доходить».

Essay 2Outline the arguments for and against continuity in religious practice between the Indus Valley and Vedic Civilisations.

¶1Paśupati seal frames the debate＋
Paśupati sealMohenjo-daroIndus Valley Civilisation (c. 2600–1900 BCE)

The Paśupati seal from Mohenjo-daro — a horned figure seated among animals — has carried the case for religious continuity between the Indus Valley Civilisation (IVC, c. 2600–1900 BCE) and Vedic India for nearly a century. Whether the seal can carry that weight is another matter.

¶2For: Marshall’s proto-Śiva＋
Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization (1931) “Lord of Beasts” John Marshall (1931)

For continuity. The strongest case was made by John Marshall in his Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization (1931), the foundational excavation report whose interpretation dominated for half a century. A horned, possibly three-faced figure sits in a yogic posture, surrounded by animals. Marshall read this as a proto-Śiva: the horns prefigure the trident, the seated posture prefigures the Mahāyogin, the surrounding animals prefigure Paśupati (“Lord of Beasts”). He added that conical stones from IVC sites resemble later liṅgas. On this reading, a deity worshipped in the IVC was absorbed into the Brahmanical tradition and resurfaces as Rudra-Śiva.

¶3Against: no link, four-century gap＋
“Unhinging Śiva from the Indus Civilization” (1983) Srinivasan (1983)B.B. Lal

Against continuity. Two problems have made Marshall’s case difficult to sustain, and since the 1980s the consensus has shifted against it. The IVC script is undeciphered, so nothing can be said directly about Indus religion, only about images. Srinivasan (“Unhinging Śiva from the Indus Civilization”, 1983) argued that the seal figure is more plausibly a horned god of fertility than a yogin, and that the conical stones are not unambiguously liṅgas. Second, there is a chronological gap. B.B. Lal places the Harappan decline around 1900 BCE and the earliest Indo-Aryan presence around 1500 BCE — a four-century interval with no overlap. Direct transmission needs people in contact; the dates do not allow it.

¶4Middle: fragmentation, partial pickup＋
Cemetery H (c. 1900–1700 BCE) Allchin & AllchinPossehl

A middle position. Allchin & Allchin and Possehl describe the IVC not as collapsing but as breaking up into smaller regional cultures (Cemetery H, c. 1900–1700 BCE). New pottery and burial customs spread through these regions slowly and peacefully, not by conquest. On this view, scattered elements of late Harappan practice may have been picked up by the incoming Vedic culture — not a whole inherited religion, but bits and pieces. This is a much weaker kind of continuity than Marshall claimed.

¶5Conclusion: no strong continuity＋
“Visual resemblance is not transmission”

Conclusion. Visual resemblance is not transmission, and the four-century gap rules out direct succession. There is no positive evidence for continuity in the strong sense, but the fragmentation model leaves room for partial absorption of post-Harappan elements into the Vedic world.

Essay 3Does the term “Hinduism” usefully capture anything before 1400 C.E.?

¶1Thesis: an outsiders’ label only＋
“the Indians who are not Muslim” al-Bīrūnī (10th c.)

The short answer: barely, and only as a word used by outsiders. “Hindu” was never the name the people themselves used. It started as a Persian word for the land beyond the Indus river. From about the tenth century, Muslim writers like al-Bīrūnī used it as a religious term only to mean “the Indians who are not Muslim”. So before 1400 the word is a label put on people from outside; it does not name a religion they felt inside.

¶2No single religion inside＋
the Veda “deceptive term” von Stietencron

The people called “Hindu” did not see themselves as one religion. They were Vaiṣṇavas, Śaivas, Śāktas and followers of the Veda — each with its own gods, texts and teachers, and often opposed to each other. This is why von Stietencron calls “Hinduism” a “deceptive term”: it makes one religion out of many. There was no single thing inside for the word to catch.

¶3Lorenzen: a “not Muslim” identity＋
“Who Invented Hinduism?” “we are not Muslim”“Sultan among the Hindu kings” Lorenzen (1999)

But the word is not empty either. Lorenzen (“Who Invented Hinduism?”) shows that under Muslim rule a “we are not Muslim” feeling slowly grew. By about 1352, kings in south India gave themselves a title that meant “Sultan among the Hindu kings” — Indians now using the word of themselves (Lorenzen 1999). So the word did start to mean something real, but only “we who are not Muslim”, not a shared faith.

¶4Conclusion: a practical outsider word＋
“a word for rulers to name and tax other people”

Does “Hinduism” then catch anything real before 1400? As the name of one religion, no — that idea came later, with the British. As a name for a group of people defined from outside, and, late and weakly, for a feeling of “not Muslim”, yes — but only that. Its real use was always practical: a word for rulers to name and tax other people, not a description of how those people saw themselves.

RUСвязь параграфов — как одно вытекает из другого＋
  • ¶1Короткий ответ: едва ли — и только как слово чужаков. «Hindu» — персидское слово для земли за Индом; с ~X в. мусульманские авторы (al-Bīrūnī) применяют его религиозно = «индийцы-немусульмане». До 1400 это ярлык извне, не самоназвание.
  • ¶1→¶2А что внутри? Ничего единого: вайшнавы, шайвы, шакты, ведийцы — свои боги, тексты, учителя, часто во вражде. Поэтому von Stietencron: «Hinduism» — «обманчивый термин», делает одну религию из многих. Ловить слову нечего.
  • ¶2→¶3Но слово и не пустое. Lorenzen («Who Invented Hinduism?»): под мусульманским правлением растёт чувство «мы не мусульмане»; ~1352 южноиндийские цари титулуют себя «султан среди индусских царей» — индийцы сами употребляют слово. Появляется реальный смысл, но только «мы не мусульмане», не общая вера.
  • ¶3→¶4Вывод. Как имя одной религии — нет (это позже, с британцами). Как имя группы извне и, поздно и слабо, чувства «не мусульмане» — да, но и только. Реальная функция — практическая: слово правителей, чтобы называть и облагать налогом других, не самоописание.

Essay 4Doctrinal limits of authority: what Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha could not cite from the Pātañjalayogaśāstra in the Mṛgendra Yogapāda-vṛtti.

¶1NK cites Patañjali, but with restraint＋
Mṛgendratantra (Yogapāda)Yogabhāṣya yujir yoge / yuja samādhau“same word, opposite system” Bhaṭṭa Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha (late 10th c.)Patañjali

Bhaṭṭa Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha (NK, late 10th-c. Kashmir) wrote a vṛtti on the Yogapāda of the Mṛgendratantra, a Śaiva Siddhānta scripture. In it he cites Patañjali, a non-Śaiva authority, by name, but with deliberate restraint. NK quotes only three sūtras — plain technical definitions of dhāraṇā and samādhi; he takes the definitions but leaves the Sāṃkhya-Yoga metaphysics behind. He rejects Patañjali on the etymology of “yoga”, deriving it from yujir yoge (“to join”) not yuja samādhau (“to be absorbed”). The first line of his vṛtti defines kaivalya, Patañjali’s central term, as what dīkṣā grants, while in the Yogabhāṣya it is the self-establishment of citi-śakti. Same word, opposite system. The two frameworks are incompatible in theology, soteriology, and the goal of yoga.

¶2Theology: creator vs one puruṣa＋
Śaiva Siddhānta Patañjali

The theological gap is the first. Śaiva Siddhānta makes Śiva the paramakāraṇa of a 36-tattva cosmos, performing the five cosmic functions (pañcakṛtya). For Patañjali, īśvara is a puruṣa-viśeṣa, one liberated being among many in a 25-tattva scheme; this īśvara is an object of meditation, not a creator. Citing him would drop Śiva to one puruṣa among many and break pañcakṛtya.

¶3Soteriology: dīkṣā vs knowledge＋
KauṇḍinyaPatañjali

The soteriologies diverge just as sharply. For NK, bondage is mala, a subtle substance covering the soul, removable only by ritual dīkṣā; knowledge does not liberate. For Patañjali, bondage lies in the kleśas — mental afflictions — and liberation comes through vivekakhyāti, discriminative knowledge. To cite him would name knowledge as the liberator, which the Saiddhāntika tradition since Kauṇḍinya denies.

¶4Goal: isolation vs lordship＋
the Mṛgendra “not isolation but lordship” Patañjali

The goal of yoga is incompatible in the same way. Patañjali’s siddhis arise from saṃyama on any chosen object; NK’s are divya, arising through a graded meditative sequence directed at objects the Mṛgendra requires the yogin to see as the body of Parameśvara. Patañjali’s kaivalya is a puruṣa alone: no body, no cosmos, no agency. NK’s yogin ascends the 36-tattva hierarchy, holds a body that has become divine, and at his chosen moment abandons even that body to dwell in śivatā with the capacity, equal to Parameśvara’s, to act as agent of the five cosmic functions. The terminal state is not isolation but lordship.

¶5Conclusion: restraint is structural＋
“structural necessity, not personal preference”

NK’s restraint is not personal preference but structural necessity. Patañjali enters the vṛtti only where the doctrine is light enough to carry across. Everywhere else, citing him would have taken the vṛtti apart.

  • ¶1NK (Кашмир, к. X в.) пишет vṛtti на Yogapāda Mṛgendratantra (Śaiva Siddhānta). Цитирует Patañjali по имени, но сдержанно: только 3 сутры (определения dhāraṇā/samādhi), без санкхья-йоговской метафизики; спорит об этимологии «yoga»; kaivalya у него = то, что даёт dīkṣā. Одно слово — противоположные системы. Несовместимы в теологии, сотериологии и цели йоги — дальше эти три оси.
  • ¶1→¶2Ось 1 — теология. У Śaiva Siddhānta Śiva = paramakāraṇa космоса из 36 таттв, делает pañcakṛtya. У Patañjali īśvara = puruṣa-viśeṣa, один из многих в 25 таттвах, объект медитации, не творец. Цитировать = низвести Śiva и сломать pañcakṛtya.
  • ¶2→¶3Ось 2 — сотериология. У NK оковы = mala, снимается только ритуальной dīkṣā; знание не освобождает. У Patañjali оковы = kleśas, освобождает vivekakhyāti (знание). Цитировать = назвать знание освободителем — что сиддхантика со времён Kauṇḍinya отрицает.

Итог — не изоляция, а владычество.
  • ¶4→¶5Вывод. Сдержанность NK — не вкус, а структурная необходимость: Patañjali входит только там, где доктрина «лёгкая» для переноса; везде ещё цитирование разобрало бы vṛtti на части.

Essay 5Pratyabhijñā: how can lifeless things be Śiva’s acts, and where does the self/other split come from?

¶1Claim: all is Śiva-consciousness＋
PratyabhijñāŚivadṛṣṭiĪśvarapratyabhijñākārikā Somānanda (c. 900–950)UtpaladevaAbhinavagupta

The Pratyabhijñā (“Recognition”) school of the non-dual Śaiva-Śākta tradition of Kashmir, founded by Somānanda (c. 900–950) in his Śivadṛṣṭi, systematised by his disciple Utpaladeva in the Īśvarapratyabhijñākārikā, and brought to its full synthesis by Abhinavagupta, makes one bold claim: everything that happens — a person’s thought, a stone’s erosion, every event in existence — expresses the agency (kartṛtva) of one Śiva-consciousness — Śiva taken not iconographically but as the universal principle of awareness itself. Two questions follow. How can lifeless things be his acts? And if all is one subject, where does the split between self and other come from?

¶2Consciousness: prakāśa–vimarśa, svātantrya＋
“whatever acts, acts by Śiva’s freedom” Utpaladeva

The answer starts with what consciousness is. Utpaladeva says it has two inseparable sides: prakāśa, the light that makes anything appear, and vimarśa, the self-awareness by which that light knows itself. A light that did not know itself would be as good as unconscious. Because consciousness knows and constitutes its own objects, it depends on nothing outside it. This absolute freedom is svātantrya, and it is the ground of all action. Whatever acts, acts by Śiva’s freedom.

¶3The stone: contraction, ābhāsa＋
Ajaḍapramātṛsiddhi UtpaladevaRatié (2009)

But a stone? Through progressive contraction (saṃkoca), Śiva’s awareness narrows: less knowledge, less freedom, less ‘I’. Matter is this contraction taken to its extreme: awareness manifesting purely as object (prameya), not as a subject capable of self-awareness. Even so, the underlying awareness does not vanish, for without it the stone could not appear at all. Utpaladeva makes the point sharply in the Ajaḍapramātṛsiddhi: insentient things (jaḍa) are not self-luminous in their own right and exist only insofar as they are manifested (ābhāsa) within consciousness (Ratié 2009). The stone is real as appearance, not as an independent centre of awareness.

¶4Self/other: the knowing triad＋
“I know this” Ratié (2007)

And the self/other split? Every cognition takes the form ‘I know this’: pramātṛ (knower), prameya (known), pramāṇa (means of knowing). Pratyabhijñā reads the triad as one svātantrya in three modes: consciousness contracted as finite knower, extended as object, acting as the cognition between. The malas make this divide seem solid, but it cracks. Another person cannot be reduced to prameya, since their appearing carries a freedom resisting being merely known; this recognition of another pramātṛ is an ūha, neither perception nor inference (Ratié 2007).

¶5Conclusion: one move, both answered＋
“Śiva is consciousness itself”

One move answers both questions. Śiva is not a being or a thing; Śiva is consciousness itself, and everything in the world is consciousness appearing in different roles. Pratyabhijñā is the moment of seeing this directly: what looked like an external object is the same consciousness in another form.

RUСвязь параграфов — как одно вытекает из другого＋
  • ¶1Школа Pratyabhijñā («узнавание»): основал Somānanda (~900–950) в Śivadṛṣṭi → систематизировал ученик Utpaladeva в Īśvarapratyabhijñākārikā → полный синтез у Abhinavagupta. Делает один смелый тезис: всё, что происходит, — действие (kartṛtva) единого Śiva-сознания (Śiva = принцип осознания, не икона). Отсюда два вопроса: как безжизненное может быть его действием? и откуда раскол «я / другой»?
  • ¶1→¶2Сначала — что такое сознание. У Utpaladeva две неразделимые стороны: prakāśa (свет явления) и vimarśa (само-осознание). Свет, не знающий себя, = бессознателен. Сознание само конституирует объекты → ни от чего не зависит = svātantrya, основа всякого действия. Всё, что действует, действует свободой Śiva.
  • ¶2→¶3Первый вопрос — камень. Через сжатие (saṃkoca) осознание сужается; материя = сжатие до предела: чистый объект (prameya). Но осознание не исчезает — иначе камень вообще не явился бы. Utpaladeva в Ajaḍapramātṛsiddhi: бесчувственное (jaḍa) не самосветно, существует лишь как ābhāsa в сознании (Ratié 2009). Камень реален как явление, не как центр осознания.
  • ¶3→¶4Второй вопрос — раскол я/другой. Всякое познание: «я знаю это» — pramātṛ / prameya / pramāṇa. Pratyabhijñā: триада — одна svātantrya в трёх режимах. Malas делают раскол плотным, но он трескается: другого нельзя свести к prameya (в его явлении есть сопротивляющаяся свобода); узнавание другого pramātṛ — это ūha, не восприятие и не вывод (Ratié 2007).
  • ¶4→¶5Вывод. Один ход отвечает на оба: Śiva — не вещь и не существо, Śiva = само сознание, а мир — это сознание в разных ролях. Pratyabhijñā — момент прямого видения этого: то, что казалось внешним объектом, есть то же сознание в другой форме.

Essay 6Nala and Yudhiṣṭhira at dice — why dharmaśāstra condemns one but not the other.

¶1Same story, opposite verdict＋
MahābhārataŚukranīti 1.283 “righteous kings” (dharmarāja)

The Mahābhārata has two famous dice games. Yudhiṣṭhira loses his kingdom at Hastināpura; Nala loses his to his brother. Both are “righteous kings” (dharmarāja), both are challenged to a game by their own relatives, both lose everything and go into exile with their wives. So we would expect the law-books to judge them alike. They do not. The nīti texts hold up Nala as a warning against gambling but say nothing against Yudhiṣṭhira. Śukranīti even names him: gambling is listed among the flaws that ruin kings, and Nala is the example (Śukranīti 1.283).

¶2Yudhiṣṭhira: duty, not flaw＋
Manusmṛti 7.213 Flemming (2021)

The usual explanation is motive. Yudhiṣṭhira did not really choose. A kṣatriya cannot refuse a challenge from his elders, so the game was a duty, not a flaw (Flemming 2021). Even staking Draupadī fits the rule that a king must save himself first, then his wife, then his wealth (Manusmṛti 7.213). His act stays inside rāja-dharma.

¶3Nala: the possession is the punishment＋
Manusmṛti 2.103 “excluded, like a Śūdra, from all that is due to twice-born persons”

Nala was possessed by the demon Kali, so he too looks not fully responsible. But dharmaśāstra does not weigh intent. The real point is that the possession was itself a punishment, for skipping his morning sandhyā ritual. Manusmṛti 2.103 says one who neglects it is “excluded, like a Śūdra, from all that is due to twice-born persons.” So Nala lost his twice-born (dvija) status.

¶4Split: who keeps caste status＋
“the law judged him as an outsider”

That is why the texts split them. Yudhiṣṭhira kept his caste and ritual standing, so his gambling could still be debated within dharma. Nala had fallen out of the twice-born order, so the law no longer protected him as a king — it judged him as an outsider. The two are not treated differently because of any contradiction in the law itself.

¶5Conclusion: hierarchy, not justice＋
“preserve hierarchy, not deliver universal justice”

So dharmaśāstra here works to preserve hierarchy, not to deliver universal justice. It excuses the ruler who keeps his status and condemns the one who loses it. This is not surprising: dharmaśāstra was written by brahmins for the twice-born, and the fate of those who fell outside that order was never really its concern.

RUСвязь параграфов — как одно вытекает из другого＋
  • ¶1Парадокс: в Mahābhārata две игры в кости. Yudhiṣṭhira и Nala — оба dharmarāja, оба вызваны роднёй, оба теряют всё и уходят в изгнание с жёнами. Ждём одинакового суда — а его нет: nīti клеймят Nala как пример (Śukranīti 1.283), о Yudhiṣṭhira молчат.
  • ¶1→¶2Обычное объяснение — мотив. Yudhiṣṭhira не выбирал: kṣatriya не может отказать вызову старших → игра = долг, не порок (Flemming 2021). Даже ставка Draupadī укладывается в правило «сначала себя, потом жену, потом имущество» (Manusmṛti 7.213). Он остаётся внутри rāja-dharma.
  • ¶2→¶3Nala тоже «не виноват» — одержим демоном Kali. Но dharmaśāstra не взвешивает намерение. Суть: сама одержимость = наказание за пропуск утренней sandhyā. Manusmṛti 2.103: пренебрёгший ею «исключён, как Śūdra, из всего, что положено дваждырождённым». Nala теряет статус dvija.

Essay 7How similar and how different are the world-views of the Pāśupatas, Siddhānta Śaivas, and the Pratyabhijñā school — and how much are they based on Sāṃkhya tenets?

¶1Shared Sāṃkhya scaffolding＋
classical Sāṃkhya Īśvarakṛṣṇa (c. 4th–5th c.)

All three schools build on the dualist scaffolding of classical Sāṃkhya (Īśvarakṛṣṇa, c. 4th–5th c. CE) — consciousness (puruṣa) and matter (prakṛti) as utterly distinct, 25 tattvas, liberation through discriminative knowledge — but each transforms it in a different direction.

¶2Pāśupatas: theistic triad, ritual＋
Pāśupatas Sāṃkhya path “impure” Kauṇḍinya

Pāśupatas (2nd–4th c. CE). They keep Sāṃkhya’s basic dualism but reorganise it around a triad: pati (the Lord Paśupati), paśu (bound souls), pāśa (bonds). Where Sāṃkhya has unconscious prakṛti and many sovereign puruṣas who fall into bondage by mistake, the Pāśupatas have a supreme Lord above subordinate souls, and “bondage” becomes a real ontological category, not a cognitive error. They borrow Sāṃkhya’s 23 evolutes as kalā but place them inside the five-part pañcārtha scheme. Liberation is duḥkhānta — the end of suffering, granted by Śiva’s grace through ritual action, not by knowledge. Kauṇḍinya explicitly rejects the Sāṃkhya path as “impure”.

¶3Siddhānta: 36 tattvas, ritual grace＋
Śaiva Siddhānta śivatulya — equal to Śiva, not identical Sanderson (1988)

Śaiva Siddhānta (5th–6th c. CE). Inherits its framework from the Pāśupatas, not directly from Sāṃkhya (Sanderson 1988). Keeps the pati/paśu/pāśa triad and expands the tattva scheme from 25 to 36 to fit a theological cosmos. The Lord, now five-faced Sadāśiva, performs five acts (pañcakṛtya): creation, maintenance, destruction, concealment, revelation. Bondage is three real fetters — mala, karma, māyā — removable only by ritual dīkṣā and divine grace (śaktipāta). Liberation is śivatulya: equality with Śiva in qualities, but not identity. The Pāśupatas’ transgressive practices are domesticated for Brahmanical householders.

¶4Pratyabhijñā: non-dual recognition＋
Pratyabhijñā SomānandaUtpaladevaAbhinavagupta

Pratyabhijñā (9th–10th c. CE). Somānanda, Utpaladeva, Abhinavagupta. Collapses dualism entirely. Reality is one Śiva-consciousness, at once light (prakāśa) and self-awareness (vimarśa), inherently dynamic — not Sāṃkhya’s passive witness. The 36 tattvas are real manifestations of Śiva’s free will (svātantrya), not an independent material evolution. Bondage is self-contraction (saṃkoca) of universal consciousness into limited individuality through three malas. Liberation is pratyabhijñā — recognition of one’s identity with Śiva. Method remains cognitive recognition, ironically close to Sāṃkhya’s own — but recognising unity, not separation.

¶5Conclusion: one skeleton, three theologies＋
“the Sāṃkhya skeleton is everywhere visible — but covered by a different theology”

Each school selectively keeps, reworks, or rejects Sāṃkhya. The Pāśupatas reframe its dualism theistically; Siddhānta expands the cosmos and ritualises the path; Pratyabhijñā dissolves the dualism but keeps the cognitive method. The Sāṃkhya skeleton is everywhere visible — but covered, in each case, by a different theology.

RUСвязь параграфов — как одно вытекает из другого＋

Essay 8Were the Yogasūtra and the Yogabhāṣya compiled together? Evidence for and against the unity of the Pātañjalayogaśāstra.

¶1Maas: one work, not two authors＋
Yogasūtra (YS)Yogabhāṣya (YB)Pātañjalayogaśāstra (PYŚ) Maas (2006, 2019)Patañjali / Vyāsa

Since the early 19th century, scholarship treated the Yogasūtra (YS) and Yogabhāṣya (YB) as the work of two authors separated by centuries — Patañjali and Vyāsa. Maas (2006, 2019) has overturned this. He argues that no independent Yogasūtra ever existed: what survives is a single work, the Pātañjalayogaśāstra (PYŚ), in which sūtra and bhāṣya were compiled together by one redactor in the late 4th or early 5th century CE.

¶2For: manuscripts, syntax, colophons＋
pravavṛte (“was composed”) Maas

The case for joint compilation. Of the 82 manuscripts Maas collated, none transmits the sūtras on their own. Apparent stand-alone YS manuscripts are scribal extractions, and they disagree on where each sūtra ends and the bhāṣya begins. The most important colophons name a single work attributed to a single author. Syntactically the two layers run on as one: the prose grammatically continues the aphorism rather than citing it. Three sūtras are introduced with the verb pravavṛte (“was composed”), suggesting older material a redactor incorporated. No sub-commentator before the 14th century treats sūtra and bhāṣya as separate works.

¶3Against: genre objection & doctrinal seams＋
Nyāya · Mīmāṃsā · Brahma sūtras abhiniveśa: “fear of death” vs “obsessive attachment” FreschiGokhaleSmith

The case against. Freschi raises a simple methodological problem: do other sūtra texts (Nyāya, Mīmāṃsā, Brahma) also circulate only with their bhāṣyas? If yes, the manuscript pattern is a convention of the genre, not proof of single authorship. No one has done that comparative study. More damaging is the doctrinal evidence. Gokhale and Smith show, independently, that the bhāṣya systematically reinterprets the sūtras through a Sāṅkhya framework absent from them. The bhāṣya conflates distinctions the sūtras keep apart, and glosses abhiniveśa as “fear of death” against the word’s standard meaning (“obsessive attachment”).

¶4Conclusion: compiled, and quietly reshaped＋
“partly composed and partly compiled” MaasGokhale & Smith

The two bodies of evidence do not contradict each other. Maas himself calls the text “partly composed and partly compiled”. The redactor gathered older aphoristic material that already carried authority, so he could not rewrite it — he used the bhāṣya as an instrument of control, reframing the sūtras within Sāṅkhya without erasing their earlier sense. The seams Gokhale and Smith identify are exactly what compilation leaves behind. The right question is therefore not whether the text was compiled together, but how many independent voices it gathered and how many it quietly reshaped.

Essay 9How significant is liṅga worship in Śaivism and popular Hinduism? Explain the myths and symbolism associated with it.

¶1Liṅga: one focus, two levels＋
“serves elaborated theology and everyday devotion through a single material focus”

The liṅga is the central object of Śaiva worship and one of the most widespread icons in Hinduism. Its significance lies in its capacity to serve both elaborated theology and everyday devotion through a single material focus.

¶2Spread: pūjā, jyotirliṅgas, Liṅgāyats＋
Śaiva Siddhānta / MantramārgaVīraśaivas (Liṅgāyats)

In Śaiva Siddhānta and the wider Mantramārga, the liṅga is the object of daily pūjā: the worshipper purifies himself, invokes Śiva into the liṅga, and offers the standard sequence of services. At the popular level, every Śaiva temple is built around a liṅga, and the twelve jyotirliṅgas form a pan-Indian network of pilgrimage. The Vīraśaivas (Liṅgāyats) of Karnataka go further: each initiate carries a small personal iṣṭaliṅga on the body, making Śiva-worship literally portable. The liṅga therefore unites the elite tantric tradition, the temple economy, and lay practice in a way no other Hindu icon does.

¶3Two myths: Liṅgodbhava, Dāruvana＋
LiṅgapurāṇaŚivapurāṇa

Two myths anchor its meaning. The Liṅgodbhava story (Liṅgapurāṇa, Śivapurāṇa) tells how Brahmā and Viṣṇu, disputing supremacy, see a pillar of fire without beginning or end. Brahmā flies up as a goose, Viṣṇu burrows down as a boar; neither finds its limit. Śiva then emerges from the column and is recognised as supreme. The Dāruvana myth tells of Śiva wandering naked through the pine forest of the sages; their wives are entranced, the sages curse him, his phallus falls. Cosmic disorder follows until the sages institute liṅga-worship as expiation.

¶4Symbolism: “sign” vs phallus debate＋
Śvetāśvatara UpaniṣadPāśupatasūtra 1.6 liṅga = “sign / mark”liṅgadhārī “bearer of the sign” DonigerKramrisch

The symbolism works on several levels and scholars disagree about which comes first. The word liṅga itself means “sign” or “mark”, and the Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad already uses it for a Śiva who is beyond all attributes, including gender. The Pāśupatasūtra (1.6) calls its practitioner liṅgadhārī — “bearer of the sign” — using liṅga as a distinguishing mark, not a fertility symbol. Doniger reads the liṅga as both phallus and its opposite — a pillar of renounced sexuality. Kramrisch goes further: the phallic shape stands for ūrdhvaretas, the celibate’s upward retention of seed, not for fertility at all.

¶5Conclusion: meanings coexist＋
“none of these meanings excludes the others”

What makes the liṅga so important is precisely that none of these meanings excludes the others. Cosmic pillar, fallen phallus, abstract sign, ascetic’s retained seed — they coexist in one object, which is why both the learned tantric and the village pilgrim can approach it.
