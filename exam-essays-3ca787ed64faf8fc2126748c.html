Essay 1“Tantric/Purāṇic Śiva cannot simply be the peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra.” Discuss.

¶1Thesis: the claim is right＋
“peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra”

The claim is right. The phrase “peaceful aspect of Vedic Rudra” gets both ends wrong — what śiva was in the Veda, and what Tantric Śiva is (I’ll keep the Purāṇic outside).

¶2Veda: Rudra is a fearsome god＋
ṚgvedaRV 1.114 · 2.33 · 7.46RV 10.92.9Taittirīya SaṃhitāŚatarudrīyaTS 5.7.3.3TS 4.5.8 “the well-disposed, very helpful, self-glorious one”“two bodies… ghora- / śiva-”namaḥ śivāya ca śivatarāya ca Jamison & BreretonSrinivasan

Rudra in the Ṛgveda is primarily a fearsome god, cattle-slayer (goghna) and man-slayer (puruṣaghna), to be begged to turn his arrows away (RV 1.114, 2.33, 7.46). Against this baseline, śiva enters as an adjective, “kindly”, “auspicious”, and the first direct application to Rudra appears only late, in RV 10.92.9, where Jamison and Brereton render the relevant phrase as “the well-disposed, very helpful, self-glorious one.” It begins as a way of speaking to a dangerous god. By the later Vedic period this hardens into a proper aspect. The Taittirīya Saṃhitā makes the structure explicit: Rudra has “two bodies, the one fearful (ghora-), the other auspicious (śiva-)”, and the Śatarudrīya is composed specifically to pacify the fearful body while a separate offering honours the auspicious one (TS 5.7.3.3; Srinivasan). The same litany addresses Rudra by the epithet itself in namaḥ śivāya ca śivatarāya ca (TS 4.5.8). So a śiva aspect does emerge, but it never detaches. It is one pole of a ritual mechanism for handling a fearsome god, always paired with the fearful body it soothes, never a separable figure in its own right.

¶3Tantras take Rudra whole＋
Śaiva SiddhāntaTrikaPratyabhijñāVidyāpīṭhaAbhinavagupta

The Tantras then do not select the peaceful side. They take Rudra whole and develop both poles into full theology. The śiva pole becomes Sadāśiva of Śaiva Siddhānta, Paramaśiva of the Trika, the absolute consciousness of the Pratyabhijñā, Śiva as light and self-awareness (prakāśa-vimarśa). The ghora pole becomes Bhairava — the fierce cremation-ground deity of the Bhairava and Vidyāpīṭha tantras, emulated in the skull-bearing Kāpālika observance. In the great non-dualist syntheses of Abhinavagupta the two are one god, held together. What was a ritual technology in the Veda becomes a theological vision in the Tantras: not the calming of a fearsome god, but the full elaboration of his double nature.

¶4Fierce pole: pre-Tantric genealogy＋
AtharvavedaAV 15.1 “ox vow” Acharya (2013)

The fierce pole, moreover, has its own pre-Tantric genealogy. The Atharvaveda records Rudra’s worship among the Vrātyas (AV 15.1), groups outside mainstream Brahmanical culture who maintained vow-based ritual traditions. Acharya (2013) traces the Pāśupata godharma back to the Vrātya “ox vow”, elaborated in more transgressive form, with imitation of bovine behaviour and suspension of dietary and sexual taboos. What the Pāśupata–Kāpālika line inherits is not yet a fully formed fierce Śiva — that theology develops later — but the formal pattern of the antinomian vow, running alongside the mainstream cult from the start.

¶5Conclusion: double-bodied, not half＋
“peaceful Rudra” never existed

Vedic Rudra is the forerunner of Tantric Śiva, but as a double-bodied god, never as half of one. A “peaceful Rudra” never existed to be carried through.

Essay 2Outline the arguments for and against continuity in religious practice between the Indus Valley and Vedic Civilisations.

¶1Paśupati seal frames the debate＋
Paśupati sealMohenjo-daroIndus Valley Civilisation (c. 2600–1900 BCE)

The Paśupati seal from Mohenjo-daro — a horned figure seated among animals — has carried the case for religious continuity between the Indus Valley Civilisation (IVC, c. 2600–1900 BCE) and Vedic India for nearly a century. Whether the seal can carry that weight is another matter.

¶2For: Marshall’s proto-Śiva＋
Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization (1931) “Lord of Beasts” John Marshall (1931)

For continuity. The strongest case was made by John Marshall in his Mohenjo-daro and the Indus Civilization (1931), the foundational excavation report whose interpretation dominated for half a century. A horned, possibly three-faced figure sits in a yogic posture, surrounded by animals. Marshall read this as a proto-Śiva: the horns prefigure the trident, the seated posture prefigures the Mahāyogin, the surrounding animals prefigure Paśupati (“Lord of Beasts”). He added that conical stones from IVC sites resemble later liṅgas. On this reading, a deity worshipped in the IVC was absorbed into the Brahmanical tradition and resurfaces as Rudra-Śiva.

¶3Against: no link, four-century gap＋
“Unhinging Śiva from the Indus Civilization” (1983) Srinivasan (1983)B.B. Lal

Against continuity. Two problems have made Marshall’s case difficult to sustain, and since the 1980s the consensus has shifted against it. The IVC script is undeciphered, so nothing can be said directly about Indus religion, only about images. Srinivasan (“Unhinging Śiva from the Indus Civilization”, 1983) argued that the seal figure is more plausibly a horned god of fertility than a yogin, and that the conical stones are not unambiguously liṅgas. Second, there is a chronological gap. B.B. Lal places the Harappan decline around 1900 BCE and the earliest Indo-Aryan presence around 1500 BCE — a four-century interval with no overlap. Direct transmission needs people in contact; the dates do not allow it.

¶4Middle: fragmentation, partial pickup＋
Cemetery H (c. 1900–1700 BCE) Allchin & AllchinPossehl

A middle position. Allchin & Allchin and Possehl describe the IVC not as collapsing but as breaking up into smaller regional cultures (Cemetery H, c. 1900–1700 BCE). New pottery and burial customs spread through these regions slowly and peacefully, not by conquest. On this view, scattered elements of late Harappan practice may have been picked up by the incoming Vedic culture — not a whole inherited religion, but bits and pieces. This is a much weaker kind of continuity than Marshall claimed.

¶5Conclusion: no strong continuity＋
“Visual resemblance is not transmission”

Conclusion. Visual resemblance is not transmission, and the four-century gap rules out direct succession. There is no positive evidence for continuity in the strong sense, but the fragmentation model leaves room for partial absorption of post-Harappan elements into the Vedic world.

Essay 3Does the term “Hinduism” usefully capture anything before 1400 C.E.?

¶1Thesis: an outsiders’ label only＋
“the Indians who are not Muslim” al-Bīrūnī (10th c.)

The short answer: barely, and only as a word used by outsiders. “Hindu” was never the name the people themselves used. It started as a Persian word for the land beyond the Indus river. From about the tenth century, Muslim writers like al-Bīrūnī used it as a religious term only to mean “the Indians who are not Muslim”. So before 1400 the word is a label put on people from outside; it does not name a religion they felt inside.

¶2No single religion inside＋
the Veda “deceptive term” von Stietencron

The people called “Hindu” did not see themselves as one religion. They were Vaiṣṇavas, Śaivas, Śāktas and followers of the Veda — each with its own gods, texts and teachers, and often opposed to each other. This is why von Stietencron calls “Hinduism” a “deceptive term”: it makes one religion out of many. There was no single thing inside for the word to catch.

¶3Lorenzen: a “not Muslim” identity＋
“Who Invented Hinduism?” “we are not Muslim”“Sultan among the Hindu kings” Lorenzen (1999)

But the word is not empty either. Lorenzen (“Who Invented Hinduism?”) shows that under Muslim rule a “we are not Muslim” feeling slowly grew. By about 1352, kings in south India gave themselves a title that meant “Sultan among the Hindu kings” — Indians now using the word of themselves (Lorenzen 1999). So the word did start to mean something real, but only “we who are not Muslim”, not a shared faith.

¶4Conclusion: a practical outsider word＋
“a word for rulers to name and tax other people”

Does “Hinduism” then catch anything real before 1400? As the name of one religion, no — that idea came later, with the British. As a name for a group of people defined from outside, and, late and weakly, for a feeling of “not Muslim”, yes — but only that. Its real use was always practical: a word for rulers to name and tax other people, not a description of how those people saw themselves.

Essay 4Doctrinal limits of authority: what Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha could not cite from the Pātañjalayogaśāstra in the Mṛgendra Yogapāda-vṛtti.

¶1NK cites Patañjali, but with restraint＋
Mṛgendratantra (Yogapāda)Yogabhāṣya yujir yoge / yuja samādhau“same word, opposite system” Bhaṭṭa Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha (late 10th c.)Patañjali

Bhaṭṭa Nārāyaṇakaṇṭha (NK, late 10th-c. Kashmir) wrote a vṛtti on the Yogapāda of the Mṛgendratantra, a Śaiva Siddhānta scripture. In it he cites Patañjali, a non-Śaiva authority, by name, but with deliberate restraint. NK quotes only three sūtras — plain technical definitions of dhāraṇā and samādhi; he takes the definitions but leaves the Sāṃkhya-Yoga metaphysics behind. He rejects Patañjali on the etymology of “yoga”, deriving it from yujir yoge (“to join”) not yuja samādhau (“to be absorbed”). The first line of his vṛtti defines kaivalya, Patañjali’s central term, as what dīkṣā grants, while in the Yogabhāṣya it is the self-establishment of citi-śakti. Same word, opposite system. The two frameworks are incompatible in theology, soteriology, and the goal of yoga.

¶2Theology: creator vs one puruṣa＋
Śaiva Siddhānta Patañjali

The theological gap is the first. Śaiva Siddhānta makes Śiva the paramakāraṇa of a 36-tattva cosmos, performing the five cosmic functions (pañcakṛtya). For Patañjali, īśvara is a puruṣa-viśeṣa, one liberated being among many in a 25-tattva scheme; this īśvara is an object of meditation, not a creator. Citing him would drop Śiva to one puruṣa among many and break pañcakṛtya.

¶3Soteriology: dīkṣā vs knowledge＋
KauṇḍinyaPatañjali

The soteriologies diverge just as sharply. For NK, bondage is mala, a subtle substance covering the soul, removable only by ritual dīkṣā; knowledge does not liberate. For Patañjali, bondage lies in the kleśas — mental afflictions — and liberation comes through vivekakhyāti, discriminative knowledge. To cite him would name knowledge as the liberator, which the Saiddhāntika tradition since Kauṇḍinya denies.

¶4Goal: isolation vs lordship＋
the Mṛgendra te samādhāv upasargāḥ (YS 3.37)“as able as Parameśvara · five functions”“not isolation but lordship” PatañjaliSanderson

For Patañjali, you gain powers (siddhis) by focusing the mind (saṃyama) on any object, and the object works on its own; he calls them obstacles only with respect to samādhi, while in the extraverted state they remain attainments (Yogasūtra 3.37). The Mṛgendra keeps the object equally open — it too can be anything — but reverses where the power comes from: an object is inert and yields nothing by itself, and bears fruit only when the yogin sees it as the body of Parameśvara (Sanderson). The powers it gives are instead divine (divya). The end-states are opposite too. Patañjali’s kaivalya is the soul completely alone: no body, no world, no action. NK’s yogin instead rises through the 36-tattva ladder, gains a body that has become divine, roams the worlds with these divine powers, and at a moment of his own choosing lets even that body go — to rest in śivatā, “just as able as Parameśvara to carry out the five cosmic functions”. The end is not isolation but lordship.

¶5Conclusion: restraint is structural＋
“structural necessity, not personal preference”

NK’s restraint is not personal preference but structural necessity. Patañjali enters the vṛtti only where the doctrine is light enough to carry across. Everywhere else, citing him would have taken the vṛtti apart.

Essay 5Pratyabhijñā: how can lifeless things be Śiva’s acts, and where does the self/other split come from?

¶1Claim: all is Śiva-consciousness＋
PratyabhijñāŚivadṛṣṭiĪśvarapratyabhijñākārikā Somānanda (c. 900–950)UtpaladevaAbhinavagupta

The Pratyabhijñā (“Recognition”) school of the non-dual Śaiva-Śākta tradition of Kashmir, founded by Somānanda (c. 900–950) in his Śivadṛṣṭi, systematised by his disciple Utpaladeva in the Īśvarapratyabhijñākārikā, and brought to its full synthesis by Abhinavagupta, makes one bold claim: everything that happens — a person’s thought, a stone’s erosion, every event in existence — expresses the agency (kartṛtva) of one Śiva-consciousness — Śiva taken not iconographically but as the universal principle of awareness itself. Two questions follow. How can lifeless things be his acts? And if all is one subject, where does the split between self and other come from?

¶2Consciousness: prakāśa–vimarśa, svātantrya＋
“whatever acts, acts by Śiva’s freedom” Utpaladeva

The answer starts with what consciousness is. Utpaladeva says it has two inseparable sides: prakāśa, the light that makes anything appear, and vimarśa, the self-awareness by which that light knows itself. A light that did not know itself would be as good as unconscious. Because consciousness knows and constitutes its own objects, it depends on nothing outside it. This absolute freedom is svātantrya, and it is the ground of all action. Whatever acts, acts by Śiva’s freedom.

¶3The stone: contraction, ābhāsa＋
Ajaḍapramātṛsiddhi UtpaladevaRatié (2009)

But a stone? Through progressive contraction (saṃkoca), Śiva’s awareness narrows: less knowledge, less freedom, less ‘I’. Matter is this contraction taken to its extreme: awareness manifesting purely as object (prameya), not as a subject capable of self-awareness. Even so, the underlying awareness does not vanish, for without it the stone could not appear at all. Utpaladeva makes the point sharply in the Ajaḍapramātṛsiddhi: insentient things (jaḍa) are not self-luminous in their own right and exist only insofar as they are manifested (ābhāsa) within consciousness (Ratié 2009). The stone is real as appearance, not as an independent centre of awareness.

¶4Self/other: the knowing triad＋
“I know this” Ratié (2007)

And the self/other split? Every cognition takes the form ‘I know this’: pramātṛ (knower), prameya (known), pramāṇa (means of knowing). Pratyabhijñā reads the triad as one svātantrya in three modes: consciousness contracted as finite knower, extended as object, acting as the cognition between. The malas make this divide seem solid, but it cracks. Another person cannot be reduced to prameya, since their appearing carries a freedom resisting being merely known; this recognition of another pramātṛ is an ūha, neither perception nor inference (Ratié 2007). What it positively is, on Ratié’s reading, is a direct intuition of the other’s own freedom (svātantrya) — the sense that they too are a self-aware subject who freely grasps themselves, and so can never be reduced to an object.

¶5Conclusion: one move, both answered＋
“one freedom contracting itself”

Both puzzles have one root. The stone and the self/other split are not two problems but one: a single freedom (svātantrya) contracting itself (saṃkoca) — into mute object at one extreme, into a limited “I” set against an “other” at the next. Pratyabhijñā is that contraction run backwards: recognising the object, and the other, as the one free consciousness they were all along. That is why it is called “recognition” — not learning something new, but knowing again what was never not the case.

Essay 6Nala and Yudhiṣṭhira at dice — why dharmaśāstra condemns one but not the other.

¶1Same story, opposite verdict＋
MahābhārataŚukranīti 1.283 “righteous kings” (dharmarāja)

The Mahābhārata has two famous dice games. Yudhiṣṭhira loses his kingdom at Hastināpura; Nala loses his to his brother. Both are “righteous kings” (dharmarāja), both are challenged to a game by their own relatives, both lose everything and go into exile with their wives. So we would expect the law-books to judge them alike. They do not. The nīti texts hold up Nala as a warning against gambling but say nothing against Yudhiṣṭhira. Śukranīti even names him: gambling is listed among the flaws that ruin kings, and Nala is the example (Śukranīti 1.283).

¶2Yudhiṣṭhira: duty, not flaw＋
Manusmṛti 7.213 Flemming (2021)

The usual explanation is motive. Yudhiṣṭhira did not really choose. A kṣatriya cannot refuse a challenge from his elders, so the game was a duty, not a flaw (Flemming 2021). Even staking Draupadī fits the rule that a king must save himself first, then his wife, then his wealth (Manusmṛti 7.213). His act stays inside rāja-dharma.

¶3Nala: the possession is the punishment＋
Manusmṛti 2.103 “excluded, like a Śūdra, from all that is due to twice-born persons”

Nala was possessed by the demon Kali, so he too looks not fully responsible. But dharmaśāstra does not weigh intent. The real point is that the possession was itself a punishment, for skipping his morning sandhyā ritual. Manusmṛti 2.103 says one who neglects it is “excluded, like a Śūdra, from all that is due to twice-born persons.” So Nala lost his twice-born (dvija) status.

¶4Split: who keeps caste status＋
“the law judged him as an outsider”

That is why the texts split them. Yudhiṣṭhira kept his caste and ritual standing, so his gambling could still be debated within dharma. Nala had fallen out of the twice-born order, so the law no longer protected him as a king — it judged him as an outsider. The two are not treated differently because of any contradiction in the law itself.

¶5Conclusion: hierarchy, not justice＋
“preserve hierarchy, not deliver universal justice”

So dharmaśāstra here works to preserve hierarchy, not to deliver universal justice. It excuses the ruler who keeps his status and condemns the one who loses it. This is not surprising: dharmaśāstra was written by brahmins for the twice-born, and the fate of those who fell outside that order was never really its concern.

Essay 7How similar and how different are the world-views of the Pāśupatas, Siddhānta Śaivas, and the Pratyabhijñā school — and how much are they based on Sāṃkhya tenets?

¶1Shared Sāṃkhya scaffolding＋
classical Sāṃkhya Īśvarakṛṣṇa (c. 4th–5th c.)

All three schools build on the dualist scaffolding of classical Sāṃkhya (Īśvarakṛṣṇa, c. 4th–5th c. CE) — consciousness (puruṣa) and matter (prakṛti) as utterly distinct, 25 tattvas, liberation through discriminative knowledge — but each transforms it in a different direction.

¶2Pāśupatas: theistic triad, ritual＋
Pāśupatas Sāṃkhya path “impure” Kauṇḍinya

Pāśupatas (2nd–4th c. CE). They keep Sāṃkhya’s basic dualism but reorganise it around a triad: pati (the Lord Paśupati), paśu (bound souls), pāśa (bonds). Where Sāṃkhya has unconscious prakṛti and many sovereign puruṣas who fall into bondage by mistake, the Pāśupatas have a supreme Lord above subordinate souls, and “bondage” becomes a real ontological category, not a cognitive error. They borrow Sāṃkhya’s 23 evolutes as kalā but place them inside the five-part pañcārtha scheme. Liberation is duḥkhānta — the end of suffering, granted by Śiva’s grace through ritual action, not by knowledge. Kauṇḍinya explicitly rejects the Sāṃkhya path as “impure”.

¶3Siddhānta: 36 tattvas, ritual grace＋
Śaiva Siddhānta śivatulya — equal to Śiva, not identical Sanderson (1988)

Śaiva Siddhānta (5th–6th c. CE). Inherits its framework from the Pāśupatas, not directly from Sāṃkhya (Sanderson 1988). Keeps the pati/paśu/pāśa triad and expands the tattva scheme from 25 to 36 to fit a theological cosmos. The Lord, now five-faced Sadāśiva, performs five acts (pañcakṛtya): creation, maintenance, destruction, concealment, revelation. Bondage is three real fetters — mala, karma, māyā — removable only by ritual dīkṣā and divine grace (śaktipāta). Liberation is śivatulya: equality with Śiva in qualities, but not identity. The Pāśupatas’ transgressive practices are domesticated for Brahmanical householders.

¶4Pratyabhijñā: non-dual recognition＋
Pratyabhijñā SomānandaUtpaladevaAbhinavagupta

Pratyabhijñā (9th–10th c. CE). Somānanda, Utpaladeva, Abhinavagupta. Collapses dualism entirely. Reality is one Śiva-consciousness, at once light (prakāśa) and self-awareness (vimarśa), inherently dynamic — not Sāṃkhya’s passive witness. The 36 tattvas are real manifestations of Śiva’s free will (svātantrya), not an independent material evolution. Bondage is self-contraction (saṃkoca) of universal consciousness into limited individuality through three malas. Liberation is pratyabhijñā — recognition of one’s identity with Śiva. Method remains cognitive recognition, ironically close to Sāṃkhya’s own — but recognising unity, not separation.

¶5Conclusion: one skeleton, three theologies＋
“the Sāṃkhya skeleton is everywhere visible — but covered by a different theology”

Each school selectively keeps, reworks, or rejects Sāṃkhya. The Pāśupatas reframe its dualism theistically; Siddhānta expands the cosmos and ritualises the path; Pratyabhijñā dissolves the dualism but keeps the cognitive method. The Sāṃkhya skeleton is everywhere visible — but covered, in each case, by a different theology.

Essay 8Were the Yogasūtra and the Yogabhāṣya compiled together? Evidence for and against the unity of the Pātañjalayogaśāstra.

¶1Maas: one work, not two authors＋
Yogasūtra (YS)Yogabhāṣya (YB)Pātañjalayogaśāstra (PYŚ) Maas (2006, 2019)Patañjali / VyāsaWhite (2014) “calling this text by the wrong name”

Since the early 19th century, scholarship treated the Yogasūtra (YS) and Yogabhāṣya (YB) as the work of two authors separated by centuries — Patañjali and Vyāsa. Maas (2006, 2019) has overturned this. He argues that no independent Yogasūtra ever existed: what survives is a single work, the Pātañjalayogaśāstra (PYŚ), in which sūtra and bhāṣya were compiled together by one redactor in the late 4th or early 5th century CE. As David Gordon White puts it, “for the past two hundred years people have been calling this text by the wrong name” (White 2014, p. 229).

¶2For: manuscripts, syntax, colophons＋
pravavṛte (“was composed”) Maas

The case for joint compilation. Of the 82 manuscripts Maas collated, none transmits the sūtras on their own; the apparent stand-alone YS manuscripts are scribal extractions, and they even disagree on where each sūtra ends and the bhāṣya begins. The most reliable colophons name one work — the Pātañjalayogaśāstra — attributed to one author, Patañjali; Vyāsa as a separate commentator shows up only in a few minor manuscripts. The two layers also read as one piece of writing: instead of quoting each sūtra and then commenting on it, the prose runs straight on from the aphorism. Three sūtras are introduced with the perfect pravavṛte (“was composed”) — the wording for older material a compiler has taken in, not lines written fresh. And no sub-commentator before the 14th century treats the sūtra and bhāṣya as two separate works; the two-author idea begins only with Mādhava (c. 1340).

¶3Against: genre objection & doctrinal seams＋
Nyāya · Mīmāṃsā · Brahma sūtras abhiniveśa: “fear of death” vs “obsessive attachment” FreschiGokhaleSmith

The case against. Freschi raises a simple methodological problem: do other sūtra texts (Nyāya, Mīmāṃsā, Brahma) also circulate only with their bhāṣyas? If yes, the manuscript pattern is a convention of the genre, not proof of single authorship. No one has done that comparative study. More damaging is the doctrinal evidence. Gokhale and Smith show, independently, that the bhāṣya reinterprets the sūtras through a Sāṅkhya framework absent from them. For instance, it glosses abhiniveśa as “fear of death” (tying it to the Sāṅkhya “blind darkness”); yet across Pāṇini, Āyurveda and Buddhist usage the word means “obsessive attachment, dogmatic clinging” — which fits the sūtra’s point that it lingers “even in a learned person” (viduṣo ’pi) far better than fear of death does (Smith 2023).

¶4Conclusion: compiled, and quietly reshaped＋
“partly composed and partly compiled” MaasGokhale & Smith

The two bodies of evidence do not contradict each other. Maas himself calls the text “partly composed and partly compiled”. The redactor gathered older aphoristic material that already carried authority, so he could not rewrite it — he used the bhāṣya as an instrument of control, reframing the sūtras within Sāṅkhya without erasing their earlier sense. The seams Gokhale and Smith identify are exactly what compilation leaves behind. The right question is therefore not whether the text was compiled together, but how many independent voices it gathered and how many it quietly reshaped.

Essay 9How significant is liṅga worship in Śaivism and popular Hinduism? Explain the myths and symbolism associated with it.

¶1Liṅga: one focus, two levels＋
“serves elaborated theology and everyday devotion through a single material focus”

The liṅga is the central object of Śaiva worship and one of the most widespread icons in Hinduism. Its significance lies in its capacity to serve both elaborated theology and everyday devotion through a single material focus.

¶2Spread: pūjā, jyotirliṅgas, Liṅgāyats＋
Śaiva Siddhānta / MantramārgaVīraśaivas (Liṅgāyats)

In Śaiva Siddhānta and the wider Mantramārga, the liṅga is the object of daily pūjā: the worshipper purifies himself, invokes Śiva into the liṅga, and offers the standard sequence of services. At the popular level, every Śaiva temple is built around a liṅga, and the twelve jyotirliṅgas form a pan-Indian network of pilgrimage. The Vīraśaivas (Liṅgāyats) of Karnataka go further: each initiate carries a small personal iṣṭaliṅga on the body, making Śiva-worship literally portable. The liṅga therefore unites the elite tantric tradition, the temple economy, and lay practice in a way no other Hindu icon does.

¶3Two myths: Liṅgodbhava, Dāruvana＋
LiṅgapurāṇaŚivapurāṇa

Two myths anchor its meaning. The Liṅgodbhava story (Liṅgapurāṇa, Śivapurāṇa) tells how Brahmā and Viṣṇu, disputing supremacy, see a pillar of fire without beginning or end. Brahmā flies up as a goose, Viṣṇu burrows down as a boar; neither finds its limit. Śiva then emerges from the column and is recognised as supreme. The Dāruvana myth tells of Śiva wandering naked through the pine forest of the sages; their wives are entranced, the sages curse him, his phallus falls. Cosmic disorder follows until the sages institute liṅga-worship as expiation.

¶4Symbolism: “sign” vs phallus debate＋
Śvetāśvatara UpaniṣadPāśupatasūtra 1.6 liṅga = “sign / mark”liṅgadhārī “bearer of the sign” DonigerKramrisch

The symbolism works on several levels and scholars disagree about which comes first. The word liṅga itself means “sign” or “mark”, and the Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad already uses it for a Śiva who is beyond all attributes, including gender. The Pāśupatasūtra (1.6) calls its practitioner liṅgadhārī — “bearer of the sign” — using liṅga as a distinguishing mark, not a fertility symbol. Doniger reads the liṅga as both phallus and its opposite — a pillar of renounced sexuality. Kramrisch goes further: the phallic shape stands for ūrdhvaretas, the celibate’s upward retention of seed, not for fertility at all.

¶5Conclusion: meanings coexist＋
“none of these meanings excludes the others”

What makes the liṅga so important is precisely that none of these meanings excludes the others. Cosmic pillar, fallen phallus, abstract sign, ascetic’s retained seed — they coexist in one object, which is why both the learned tantric and the village pilgrim can approach it.

Essay 10History of tantric Ṣaḍaṅga Yoga: its six ancillaries, and how it differs from eightfold Pātañjala Yoga.

¶1Six-limb yoga: attested, origins contested＋
Mālinīvijayottara (MVUT)GuhyasamājaMantramārga

Tantric ṣaḍaṅgayoga — the yoga of six ancillaries — is attested across early Śaiva Mantramārga scriptures, most prominently the Mālinīvijayottara (MVUT), and in Buddhist tantric traditions, notably the Guhyasamāja. Its origins, however, remain contested.

¶2Origins: no single straight line＋
Maitri UpaniṣadGuhyasamāja Mallinson (2014)Vasudeva Mallinson 2014: 8th–9th c. → no earlier than MVUT“Pāśupata prehistory… predates Patañjali”

Origins. The traditional answer points to the Maitri Upaniṣad, whose six limbs were long treated as the proto-source for everyone. But Mallinson (2014) dates this section to the 8th–9th c. CE — no earlier than the MVUT or Guhyasamāja themselves — removing it as a clear source. A second possibility, from Vasudeva, is a Pāśupata prehistory: the Mantramārga Śaivism behind the MVUT traces its yoga to Pāśupata roots that predate Patañjali — which both helps explain why this six-limb yoga diverges so sharply from Patañjali’s eightfold scheme, and points to a lost Pāśupata text as the likeliest common source (none survives). With techniques shared freely across Śaiva and Buddhist sects, there is no single straight line of descent.

¶3The six ancillaries (≠ Patañjali’s)＋
prāṇāyāma · dhāraṇā · tarka · dhyāna · samādhi · pratyāhāradhāraṇā = tantric tattva-conquesttarka = highest aṅga, links two phases MV 17.18cdVasudeva

The six ancillaries in MVUT, in order, are: prāṇāyāma (breath control), dhāraṇā (fixation), tarka (reasoning/discrimination), dhyāna (meditative visualisation), samādhi (absorption), and pratyāhāra (withdrawal of the senses) (Vasudeva) — note pratyāhāra unusually last. Five share names with Patañjali’s aṣṭāṅga, though they are defined substantively differently — dhāraṇā, for instance, denotes tantric tattva-conquest rather than mere mental fixation. Tarka in particular is singled out as the highest ancillary — “the most excellent aid to yoga” (MV 17.18cd) — the discriminative limb that links the two phases of the practice, first stabilising the mind and then absorbing it.

¶4Three differences from Pātañjala yoga＋
Kālacakra omits yama · niyama · āsanaadds tarka (= anusmṛti) Vasudeva

The differences from Pātañjala yoga are threefold. First, ṣaḍaṅgayoga omits yama, niyama, and āsana — Patañjali’s ethical-postural preliminaries. Second, it introduces tarka — discriminative reasoning, equivalent to anusmṛti in the Buddhist Kālacakra variant (Vasudeva) — which has no Pātañjala counterpart. Third, the ordering and metaphysical context differ: the tantric scheme operates within a non-dual Śaiva or Vajrayāna cosmology, whereas Patañjali presupposes Sāṃkhya dualism.

Essay 11How is the scheme of thirty-six tattvas in Śaivism not in conflict with the three principles pati, paśu, and pāśa?

¶1Two schemes: soteriology vs ontology＋
padārtha = soteriological · tattva = ontological

Within Śaivism, the thirty-six tattvas and the triad of pati, paśu, pāśa describe different aspects of doctrine and are designed to fit together rather than to compete. The padārtha-scheme is soteriological: it sorts everything that matters for liberation into three categories — the Lord (pati), the bound soul (paśu), and the bonds (pāśa). The tattva-scheme is ontological: it lists the thirty-six principles that make up reality.

¶2Pati ≠ śiva-tattva (tattvātīta)＋
Tattvaprakāśa, kārikā 25 Śiva & Śakti are tattvātīta AghoraśivaBhojadeva

Pati is not the same as śiva-tattva. Aghoraśiva, commenting on Bhojadeva’s Tattvaprakāśa kārikā 25, says that Śiva and Śakti are tattvātīta — beyond the tattvas. The Lord stands above the whole scheme. He acts through the top five tattvas — the śuddhādhvan (śiva, śakti, sadāśiva, īśvara, śuddhavidyā) — which are his manifestations, not himself.

¶3Paśu = puruṣa + five kañcukas＋
paśu = puruṣa (12), wrapped in kañcukas (7–11)

Paśu is the soul in its bound state. At the level of tattvas, it corresponds to puruṣa (tattva 12), the soul already wrapped in the five kañcukas (tattvas 7 to 11). These kañcukas shrink the Śiva-like powers of the soul into the limited capacities of an ordinary being.

¶4Pāśa: only māyā is a tattva＋
mala & karman are not tattvas Davis

Pāśa is more complex. Davis explains that the soul is bound by three different things, each working in its own way: mala, karman, māyā. Only māyā appears in the tattva-scheme. And even māyā, in its undifferentiated form, does not bind the soul on its own. What actually binds the soul is the thirty-one tattvas that come out of māyā. Mala and karman are not tattvas at all.

¶5Conclusion: one reality, two angles＋
“the schemes describe one reality from two angles”

So the schemes describe different things. The padārthas tell the practitioner what categories of reality matter for liberation; the tattvas describe how the manifest cosmos that makes up most of pāśa is internally structured. Pati relates to the whole through his five activities, especially concealment (tirobhāva) and grace (anugraha), which set the fetters in motion and then dissolve them in initiation. The schemes describe one reality from two angles.

Essay 12“Yoga is the cessation of the operations of the mind.” Discuss the principles underlying this definition.

¶1Two sūtras: a state or an act?＋
Yoga-sūtra 1.1–1.2 cittavṛttinirodha“yoga is absorption (samādhi)” Vyāsa

The first two sūtras of the Yoga-sūtra seem to clash. Vyāsa glosses the first as “yoga is absorption” (samādhi) — a state you are in. The second says yoga is the stilling of the mind’s activities (cittavṛttinirodha) — something you do. So is yoga a state or an act? Vyāsa says both: the second sūtra simply unpacks the first.

¶2Principle 1: mind = three guṇas＋
“settle the mind, do not destroy it”

The first principle behind this is what the mind is made of. The mind has three strands (guṇa): sattva, rajas, tamas. What the mind does at any moment depends on which strand is on top. So the mind’s “activities” are just these three strands at play. To still the activities is to calm rajas and tamas so that sattva rises: you settle the mind, you do not destroy it.

¶3Principle 2: stilling in stages＋
“the seer is not the mind”

The second principle is that this happens in stages. As rajas and tamas fade and sattva grows, the mind moves up: from ordinary thinking, through states with knowledge, detachment and power, to a point where it rests in itself. At the top, one clear seeing remains — the seer is not the mind. Then even that is let go.

¶4Two levels: saṃprajñāta → nirbīja＋
saṃprajñāta (with object)asaṃprajñāta / nirbīja (seedless)

From this follows the central point: stilling has two levels. In the first, the mind still holds an object. Because sūtra 1.1 takes “yoga” in its widest sense, even this “with-knowing” absorption (saṃprajñāta) counts. In the second, no object is left, only faint traces. This is “seedless” absorption (asaṃprajñāta / nirbīja) — stilling in the full sense.

¶5Conclusion: one line, whole path＋
“one short line covers the whole path”

So the second sūtra does not contradict the first; it unpacks it. “Stilling the mind’s activities” is a single path: at its lower end, absorption with an object; at its summit, absorption without one, where pure consciousness stands alone. Three principles hold the definition together: the mind is made of three strands; its activities quieten in stages; and the process has two levels, with-knowing rising into without-knowing. One short line covers the whole path.

Essay 13The Vijñānabhairavatantra’s meditation techniques.

¶1112 dhāraṇās — many doors, one goal＋
Vijñānabhairavatantra “their variety is the point”upāya → Bhairava (vijñāna)

The Vijñānabhairavatantra is a non-dual Trika Śaiva text in which Bhairava answers the Goddess’s question about his true nature. He answers not with doctrine but with practice: about 112 short meditations (dhāraṇās), each a different door to the same goal. Their variety is the point. Some work on the breath and the still gap between in- and out-breath; some use a mantra or inner sound; some focus on emptiness, on light, or on strong feeling — fear, wonder, sexual joy; some use the senses; some imagine the body dissolving in fire. Every method is a means (upāya) of slipping past the thinking mind into Bhairava — pure awareness (vijñāna) itself.

¶2The burning (dahana-dhāraṇā)＋
kālāgni — “fire of time”

The burning (dahana-dhāraṇā). Here one imagines the “fire of time” (kālāgni) rising from the big toe and slowly consuming the whole body, until nothing of it is left. The text says that when the body has been burnt away by this fire, peace dawns. By watching the body burn, the meditator loses the deep habit of feeling like a body. What is left, when the burning is complete, is not nothing — it is the calm, objectless awareness that is Bhairava.

¶3The void (śūnya-dhāraṇā)＋
“the Great Void” = Bhairava

The void (śūnya-dhāraṇā). A very different method reaches the same place through emptiness. One contemplates the body, the senses and the whole world as empty space — even for a single moment, and without any thought. When the objects are gone, awareness has nothing to hold on to. Emptied, it recognises itself as the Great Void, which the text identifies with Bhairava.

¶4Opposite methods, same job＋
“many doors on purpose” Silburn (1961)Singh (1979)

The two methods look opposite — one burns, the other empties — but they do the same job. Each removes everything from awareness (the body in one case, the world in the other) until only pure consciousness is left. That is the logic of the whole text. The methods differ so widely because people differ; the Vijñānabhairava offers many doors on purpose, so that everyone can find the one that opens for them onto the same Bhairava-awareness. This is why the Kashmirian non-dual tradition valued the text so highly (Silburn 1961; Singh 1979).

Essay 14To what extent is the Amṛtasiddhi Buddhist? How useful is it to classify it as such?

¶1Amṛtasiddhi: earliest haṭha source＋
Amṛtasiddhi (11th c., Deccan) mahāmudrā · mahābandha · mahāvedha

The Amṛtasiddhi (11th c., Deccan) is the earliest systematic Sanskrit treatise on physical yoga and the source text from which later haṭhayoga texts borrow verses, concepts and the three key techniques (mahāmudrā, mahābandha, mahāvedha). The essay asks two questions: how Buddhist is it, and how useful is the label?

¶2Buddhist by birth, trans-sectarian by nature＋
“unobtrusively Buddhist”Buddhist by birth, trans-sectarian by nature SchaefferMallinsonTörzsök

Buddhist features. Schaeffer first read the text as a hybrid, neither clearly Buddhist nor Śaiva. Mallinson disagrees: in its earliest form the Amṛtasiddhi is straightforwardly Vajrayāna Buddhist. The signs are clear. It opens with an invocation of the Buddhist goddess Chinnamastā. It works with four elements rather than the five used in Śaiva systems (no ether). It teaches the four blisses, a distinctively Vajrayāna doctrine. Its vocabulary is Buddhist: mahāmudrā (the great seal), vajrapañjara (the diamond cage), trikāya (the three bodies of the Buddha), śūnya (emptiness). The Śaiva-looking elements (jīvanmukti, identification with Śiva) come from later editing. Törzsök adds the key nuance. Everyone agrees the text is Buddhist; her point is that its author, Mādhavacandra, made that Buddhism deliberately quiet — she calls the text “unobtrusively Buddhist”. He chose words a Buddhist and a non-Buddhist could each read in their own way (e.g. bindu is equated at once with Buddha, Śiva, Viṣṇu and Brahmā, so every reader finds their own supreme there). Her contribution is to show that this cross-sectarian portability was built in by the author from the start, not something that only happened later as the text was copied and re-used: the Amṛtasiddhi was designed to travel. Mallinson’s work on Kadri in Mangalore shows where this could happen: a site where Vajrayāna statues were taken into Śaiva worship. The Amṛtasiddhi travelled two ways at once — into Śaiva haṭhayoga in India, and into Tibet, where it stayed Buddhist. So it is Buddhist by birth, trans-sectarian by nature: its practical core could be separated from its doctrinal frame.

¶3Is the label useful? Depends who asks＋
complicates Sanderson’s one-way model Sanderson

Is the label useful? It depends who is asking. For historians of religion, decisively yes: it overturns the old view that haṭhayoga developed from Śaivism, and complicates Sanderson’s one-way model where Śaivas are always sources and Buddhists always borrowers. For Buddhists today, it lets them claim back a practice the whole world now treats as Hindu — yoga. For living Śaiva and Nātha traditions, the label is largely irrelevant: their authority rests on lineage and practice, not on who first wrote the treatise. For method-focused practitioners, sectarian origin adds little — what matters is the transplantability itself.

Essay 15What is special about the tantric mode of worship? How far is it internal/external, for oneself/for others?

¶1Self-divinisation precedes worship＋
“only a Śiva can worship Śiva”bhūtaśuddhi

Tantric worship (pūjā) differs from Vedic or temple worship in one decisive way: you cannot approach Śiva as an ordinary man. The worshipper must first become “a Śiva” himself — by mantra and by the purification of the body’s elements (bhūtaśuddhi) — since “only a Śiva can worship Śiva”. In dualist Śaiva Siddhānta he becomes a Śiva, equal to the supreme Lord but still distinct from him; the non-dualist traditions read the same act as recognising an identity that was always there. The whole system is esoteric, open only to the initiated, and it aims at both liberation and worldly power.

¶2Internal worship ranked above external＋
“is Śiva for the duration of the cult” Brunner

Internal or external? Inner worship comes first. The fullest pūjā is done mentally, inside the body, with the senses themselves serving as offerings. This is ranked higher than worship with an image, a liṅga, a maṇḍala or a fire — these are kept only as supports. Brunner notes that the form of the image makes no real difference to the cult, because the worshipper himself “is Śiva for the duration of the cult”.

¶3ātmārtha vs parārtha — fusion of two cults＋
“a fusion of two originally separate cults”ātmārtha: dīkṣā · parārtha: ācāryābhiṣeka (Ādiśaiva) Brunner

For oneself or for others? The distinction is technical: in ātmārthapūjā the fruit of the rite goes to the worshipper himself; in parārthapūjā it goes to someone else. The aims really differ. Private worship is for one person — his own liberation, or siddhi if he is a sādhaka. Temple worship is performed for the good of the whole country, with the prince counted only as one beneficiary among many. Brunner stresses that the ritual is not really about the king at all — supporting his rule is just a by-product. The qualifications differ too: dīkṣā is enough for ātmārtha, but parārtha needs consecration as a master (ācāryābhiṣeka), and the priest must be a Brāhmaṇa and an Ādiśaiva. Brunner adds a key point: the single Āgamic model is not one original cult with a later offshoot, but a fusion of two originally separate cults — private and public — whose earlier shapes are now almost lost.

¶4Conclusion: the special combination＋
self-divinisation + esoteric + liberation & power + internal + one form, two ends

What is special is the combination: self-divinisation as the precondition for worship, reserved to the initiated, aiming at liberation and power together, primarily internal, and with one ritual form serving either the worshipper’s own benefit or the wider good — a unified model that is itself the historical fusion of a private and a public cult.

  • ¶3→¶4Вывод: особость — в комбинации: само-обожествление как условие культа, эзотеричность, освобождение+сила вместе, преимущественно внутреннее, и одна ритуальная форма — либо себе, либо ради общего блага; единая модель = историческое слияние частного и публичного культов.

Essay 16Compare the ṣaḍaṅga yogas of the Mālinīvijayottaratantra and the Vivekamārtaṇḍa, and comment on their differences.

¶1Two six-limb systems, sharply different＋
Mālinīvijayottaratantra (MVT, 9–10c)Vivekamārtaṇḍa (VM, 12–13c) “Patañjali has never been important within the practical yoga tradition” Mallinson & Singleton

The essay compares two medieval six-limbed yoga systems: the Mālinīvijayottaratantra (MVT, 9th–10th c., Kashmir) and the Vivekamārtaṇḍa (VM, 12th–13th c., Rajasthan & the Deccan). Both adopt ṣaḍaṅgayoga rather than Patañjali’s aṣṭāṅga — unsurprising, since Patañjali “has never been important within the practical yoga tradition” (Mallinson & Singleton). Beneath this shared structure the two systems differ sharply in limb selection, sequence, audience, technique, and goal.

¶2Origins: shared freely, not one straight line＋
Maitrāyaṇīya Upaniṣad 6.18Amṛtasiddhi Mallinson (2014)VasudevaMallinson (2025) Mallinson 2014: 8th–9th c. → no earlier than MVTlost Pāśupata source — none survives

Origins. The traditional answer points to the Maitrāyaṇīya Upaniṣad (6.18), whose six limbs are close to the MVT’s set and was long treated as the proto-source for everyone. But Mallinson (2014) dates this section to the 8th–9th c. CE — no earlier than the MVT itself, removing it as a clear source. A second possibility, suggested by Vasudeva and developed by Mallinson (2025), is a Pāśupata prehistory: both the Mantramārga Śaivism behind the MVT and the Nāth tradition behind the VM trace their yoga back to Pāśupata roots. A lost Pāśupata text could be the common source — but none survives. A third complication: the VM borrows verses directly from the Buddhist Amṛtasiddhi. So the techniques were shared freely between sects, not handed down in one straight line.

¶3MVT: tarka, for Trika adepts＋
MV 17.18cd order: prāṇāyāma · dhāraṇā · tarka · dhyāna · samādhi · pratyāhāratarka = “the most excellent ancillary to yoga”tattvajaya

MVT — six limbs for Trika adepts. Written for initiated Trika adepts, the MVT’s ṣaḍaṅga is auxiliary to its real goal, tattvajaya (“conquest of the reality-levels”). Its order: (1) prāṇāyāma (calm the breath to calm the mind), (2) dhāraṇā (fix the mind on a support), (3) tarka — its distinctive limb, discriminative judgment, “the most excellent ancillary to yoga” (MV 17.18cd), placed in the middle as the bridge from stabilising the mind to absorbing it — (4) dhyāna (dwell on that goal), (5) samādhi (full absorption in it), and (6) pratyāhāra, which the MVT unusually puts last, as the result of samādhi rather than a preliminary.

¶4VM: āsana, for haṭha ascetics＋
order: āsana · prāṇasaṃrodha · pratyāhāra · dhāraṇā · dhyāna · samādhi“the cheating of death” (kālasya vañcanam)samādhi: lies “as though dead” Mallinson & Singleton

VM — six limbs as the whole path. For haṭha ascetics the six limbs are not auxiliary but the path itself, aimed at liberation and “the cheating of death” (kālasya vañcanam). Its order: (1) āsana — posture, which opens the sequence (the MVT has none) but gets only siddhāsana and padmāsana; (2) prāṇasaṃrodha, which produces udghāta, an upward “eruption” of vital energy; (3) pratyāhāra, here uniquely including viparītakaraṇī; (4) dhāraṇā, (5) dhyāna, and (6) samādhi — twelve hours of breath-retention until the yogin lies “as though dead” (Mallinson & Singleton). The swap of limbs is telling: the VM adds a bodily limb (āsana), the MVT an intellectual one (tarka) — each mirrors its path, and the goal differs too: identity with Śiva (MVT) vs a deathless, powerful body (VM).

¶5Conclusion: different worlds, the same miracle＋
MVT (9–10th c.) → initiated Kashmir Śaivas; VM (later) → Deccan haṭha ascetics limbs vary with time/tradition/audience — the miraculous endures

The two yogas differ because they were built for different worlds: the MVT (9th–10th c.) for initiated Kashmir Śaivas, the VM, centuries later, for Deccan haṭha ascetics. Each fits the same six-limb frame to its own time, tradition, and audience — and to its own goal: gnostic union with Śiva, or a deathless body. The limbs vary, but the miraculous endures.

Essay 17What is the role of initiation in Tantrism and what are its procedures? What is the hierarchy of initiates?

¶1Dīkṣā: the line insider/outsider＋
Parākhyatantra 15.10Niśvāsatattvasaṃhitā dā- “to give” + kṣi- “to destroy”confers śivatva

In Śaiva and Śākta Tantra, dīkṣā (initiation) is the line between insider and outsider. Only the initiated may use mantras and pursue liberation. It claims to burn away the bonds (pāśa) that trap the soul and to confer śivatva, the nature of Śiva. The Parākhyatantra (15.10) reads the word as dā- “to give” and kṣi- “to destroy”, and the Niśvāsatattvasaṃhitā promises that bonds burned by initiation do not grow back, so that when the body falls away, the initiate goes to Śiva. Dīkṣā thus does two things: it destroys the karma that binds the soul to rebirth, and it brings the initiate into a lineage.

¶2What it does: transform vs uncover＋
“the rite uncovers; it does not create” Davis

What initiation is understood to do, however, varies from tradition to tradition. For dualist Śaiva Siddhānta, it actually transforms the soul, working a real change on a real bondage. For Abhinavagupta and the Kashmiri non-dualists, it only removes what hides a truth already there — that the soul is Śiva (Davis). The rite uncovers; it does not create.

¶3Four grades of initiate＋
NiśvāsaBrahmayāmala samayin · putraka · sādhaka · ācārya

This basic promise stays the same, but the ranks and rituals change over time. The early Niśvāsa has only sādhakas and a few ācāryas. The Brahmayāmala sorts practitioners by purity and even initiates women fully (sādhakī). Later Śaiva Siddhānta settles on four grades: the samayin (a beginner under vows), the putraka (“son”, who will be freed), the sādhaka (who seeks powers), and the ācārya (the only one who may initiate others).

¶4Procedures: simple → elaborate; vedhadīkṣā＋
adhivāsa (day-before preparation)vedhadīkṣā — power sent straight in Brunner-Lachaux

Procedures grow too. Early initiation is short and simple. Siddhānta builds it up: a long preparation the day before (adhivāsa) with a maṇḍala, an initiation cord, and bodily rites; then the guru “cuts” and purifies the soul’s bonds with fire-offering and mantra (Brunner-Lachaux). If a disciple is too weak to keep the rules — a child, a sick or old person — a simpler form still grants liberation. The Kaula teachers go the other way: Abhinavagupta’s vedhadīkṣā sends power straight into the disciple, who shakes or feels bliss as proof.

¶5Conclusion: goal fixed, proof differs＋
Siddhānta: visible ritual · Kashmir: śaktipāta

So the goal of dīkṣā never changes. What changes is how each school proves it works — Siddhānta through visible ritual, Kashmir through the “descent of power” (śaktipāta).
